# Development Retrospective

## Project Context

This project was my first foray into large-scale independant development. Up until this point, the extent of my work had been the sort of trivial, limited problems you are asked to solve for programming assignments in college.
At the project's inception, I had planned on it to be a fully fledged campaign-based game, complete with a suite of levels, optional challenges, and secrets. This proved to be a signifigant overscope for a one man team, and the game was eventually downsized to a small combat protoype. The final form of the game is feature complete and high quality, but short on overall content.
I began work on the project in late 2024, my sophomore year of college. A friend of mine would sketch out concept art for enemies and weapons, but beyond that every single asset in the game was made by me in full.

## What Went Well

I'm very satisfied with how the game feels to play. Moving around is deeply satsifying, with a large room for skill expression; at points I think the prototype even surpasses it's inspirations, something I'm deeply happy with.
I look back at the weapons with satisfaction as well. They each occupy distinct mechanical roles in the game, while feeling like a natural pairing within combat. As a programmer, they also are pretty technically distinct from one another, which is always a benefit.
Generally, I'm happy with everything contained within the final project; I'm beyond proud of it as a first excursion into indipendant development.

TLDR:
- Player movement
- Player weapons
- Game-feel and feedback
- Enemy design

## Biggest Challenges

With this project, I made the classic mistake of learning as I build. This resulted in the player class becoming a bit of a mal-designed mess towards the end of development. I suspect that if I continued development, I would have had to rewrite the player class from scratch at some point. A secondary challenge was scoping the project, which eventually curtailed it's development. 

TLDR:
- Player class design
- Overscoping

## What I Would Do Differently

If I were to build this project again, I would do three things differently:
- Plan the architecture of the player class before I write it
- Constrain mutually exlusive states via enums rather than a large number of booleans
- Plan a realistic scope right from the start

## What I Learned

When I started this project, I was what I would call an above average programmer amongst my college peers. This project shot my programming skills to the moon, such that I was never challenged by programming again during my time in college. 

I Learned:
- Unreal Engine architecture and design
- C++ Design principles
- C++ Class design
- C++ AI programming
- Unreal Engine's life cycle
- 3D Modeling
- 3D Animation
- Sound design and integration
- Unreal Engine's Niagara particle system
- Project scoping

## Final Thoughts

Going into the project, I was an inexperienced engineer, prone to overscoping, poor design, and shoddy understanding of C++. Walking out of the project, I knew my way around Unreal Enigne, proper C++ design, and a deepened understanding of proper scope. I have grown as an engineer in all possible ways, and am deeply proud of the work I put together, even if it's final scope was much restrained than the inital design.
